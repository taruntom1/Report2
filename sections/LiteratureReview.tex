\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Introduction to LoRa in Vehicular Environments}

Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) have been extensively developed around Dedicated Short-Range
Communications (DSRC, IEEE 802.11p) and Cellular V2X (C-V2X), both of which are purpose-built for
vehicular communication and remain central in standards-oriented deployments \cite{secure_iov,lorawan_v2roadside}.
Accordingly, the present work does not argue that LoRa is intrinsically better than these technologies
for mainstream V2V. Instead, this project adopts LoRa primarily for practical reasons: hardware
availability in our context and substantially lower implementation cost for prototyping.

Prior literature shows that LoRa can still be a useful experimental platform for studying low-rate vehicular
dissemination over sub-GHz links, including under mobility, when protocol design is constraint-aware
\cite{exp_study_lora_vehicle,lora_arch_v2x_move,urban_vanets_2023}. LoRa's Chirp Spread Spectrum operation
and high receiver sensitivity can support wide-area packet exchange, but low data rate, duty-cycle limits,
and long Time-on-Air impose strict efficiency requirements on forwarding behavior \cite{lora_mesh_iot,routing_mesh_2020}.
Accordingly, relevant LoRa-V2V work emphasizes selective, low-overhead safety dissemination rather than
high-throughput communication.

\subsection{The Paradigm of Centralized vs. Decentralized V2V}

A recurring theme in the V2X literature is the dependence of many practical architectures on supporting
infrastructure such as RSUs, gateways, or cellular coordination entities \cite{secure_iov,lorawan_v2roadside}.
Such architectures are appropriate for planned deployments, but they do not directly answer a different
research question: how much cooperative safety dissemination can be sustained when vehicles rely only on
peer-to-peer forwarding and local state.

This question is important in scenarios where infrastructure may be sparse, temporarily unavailable, or
outside project-scale deployment budgets. Prior studies in LoRa mesh and emergency communication indicate
the feasibility of infrastructure-free operation, yet practical evidence for fully decentralized vehicular
behavior under realistic forwarding constraints remains limited \cite{lora_mesh_emergency,lora_mesh_iot,routing_mesh_2020}.

\subsection{Evaluating Existing Network and Link Layer Protocols}

At the link layer, LoRa-focused literature consistently reports that channel access policy is decisive under
broadcast-heavy traffic. Uncoordinated access can increase collision probability, while tightly scheduled
alternatives can add synchronization burden that is hard to sustain in ad-hoc mobility
\cite{lora_mesh_iot,routing_mesh_2020}. This motivates lightweight access control mechanisms that remain
implementable on resource-constrained nodes without centralized timing support.

At the network layer, prior work highlights two common failure modes in low-bandwidth vehicular meshes:
excess control overhead from route-centric protocols in fast-changing topologies, and broadcast storms from
unrestricted flooding \cite{routing_mesh_2020,vdtn_routing_eval}. The literature therefore trends toward
managed dissemination with explicit controls such as hop limits, duplicate suppression, and forwarding
deferral to reduce redundant rebroadcast while preserving reach \cite{lora_reliable_v2v_2025,lora_mesh_emergency}.

These findings motivate the design direction used in this project: lightweight channel-aware transmission at
the link layer and controlled flooding at the network layer, avoiding route-table dependence.

\subsection{Research Gap and Proposed Architecture}

Based on the reviewed work, the key gap targeted here is the limited end-to-end validation of a fully
decentralized LoRa-based vehicular mesh that can operate without RSUs, fixed gateways, or centralized
scheduling/control in a final-year project-scale implementation \cite{lora_reliable_v2v_2025,routing_mesh_2020}.

The architecture presented in this report addresses that gap using a three-layer design implemented on
ESP32-class nodes: safety-message generation at the application layer, duplicate-aware and hop-bounded
dissemination at the network layer, and channel-aware radio framing at the link layer. The implementation is
validated through both real-node testing and deterministic simulation, enabling assessment of decentralized
forwarding behavior under controlled and field-like conditions \cite{lora_mesh_emergency,lora_reliable_v2v_2025}.
